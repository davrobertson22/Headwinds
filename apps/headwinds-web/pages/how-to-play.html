How to Play Headwinds

Headwinds is a free, browser-based multiplayer airline game. You join a world shared with other real people and grow a startup carrier into a global airline — while a server clock ticks the whole world forward, online or not. This guide takes you from sign-in to a working airline.

There's nothing to download. You sign in once, join a world, and start flying. The one thing to understand before anything else: every other airline on the map belongs to a real person. There are no computer opponents to farm — your rivals scout, adapt, form alliances, and hold grudges, and the world doesn't pause when you close the tab.

What you're playing for

Each Headwinds world runs for a fixed span of game-years and then ends with final standings. The goal is to sit at the top of that table when the clock runs out — the biggest, healthiest, most valuable airline, built without going bankrupt along the way. Because there's no AI to beat, finishing first means you out-flew actual people. Weekly rankings and in-game board objectives give you shorter targets to chase on the way there.

Getting into a world

1

Sign in

Sign in with a Google account or an email sign-in link — no password to invent. One account carries all your airlines, across every world you join and every device you play on, so you can start on a laptop and check in from a phone. Your email is only ever used to identify your account; other players never see it.

2

Pick a world and found your airline

Browse the open worlds and join one. Some are already running with rivals in flight; others are waiting in a lobby and start the moment enough players arrive. When you join, you name your airline and choose a home airport — your first hub. That choice shapes your whole early game, so it's worth a moment's thought (more on that below).

3

Learn the world clock

This is what makes Headwinds different from a solo tycoon game: time runs on the server, not on your turns. A game week passes roughly every hour, continuously, whether you're watching or asleep. You don't grind through weeks — you set up a network, let it run, and come back to a debrief of what happened while you were gone.

Your first moves

1

Choose a hub with room to breathe

Before you commit, look at where rivals are already established. A mid-size city with strong local demand and no entrenched competitor is usually a better home than the world's busiest airport, where you'd be fighting established players from your first week. Early on, empty space is worth more than prestige.

2

Get aircraft flying immediately

Your first aircraft are delivered right away, so you can open routes from the moment you found your airline instead of waiting on a delivery queue. Use them to prove a couple of markets, then lease more to match the missions you actually fly — small regional aircraft for short, thin routes; single-aisle jets for busy corridors. Don't lease long-range metal you'll only fly on short hops.

3

Open routes into open demand

Start with two or three routes out of your hub. The best early targets are city pairs with healthy demand that no rival is serving yet — claim them before someone else does. Price each route to clear its costs with room to spare, then watch your load factor: above 80% and it's working; stuck near 40% and it's bleeding you.

4

Let the clock run, then read the debrief

Step away and let a few game weeks pass. When you return, review what changed: which routes earned, which lost, where your cash landed, and — crucially — what your rivals did. Maybe a competitor opened against you; maybe one abandoned a market you can now take. This read-and-respond loop is the core of the game.

5

Respond to your rivals

When another player moves onto one of your routes, the passengers split between you based on fare, frequency, service, and loyalty. You can defend the route by matching fares and adding flights, differentiate with more departures, or redeploy that aircraft somewhere you have the edge. Choosing well is most of the skill.

Numbers to watch

A handful of figures tell you where you stand — against the market and against the people in it:

  • Cash — your runway. It funds leases, fuel, and staff while routes mature. Keep a cushion; the clock won't wait while you scramble.
  • Load factor — the share of seats you're selling. High load means your fares and capacity fit demand; chronic low load means too many seats, too high a fare, or a market that isn't there.
  • Standings & market value — your rank among the real airlines in the world, and what your airline is worth. This is the scoreboard, and it updates as rivals grow too.
  • Who's on your routes — the rival views show which players fly where. Knowing who you're up against is half of deciding what to do about them.
Tip: because the world never pauses, the strongest early habit is building a network that runs cleanly without you — then checking in to steer, not to babysit.

Because your rivals are people

Headwinds ships with an in-game messaging system and player-run alliances, and they matter as much as your route map. You can strike a truce over a contested market, carve up a region with partners, or simply talk trash across the standings. Deals aren't enforced by the game — a rival can always walk away or turn on you — which makes trust its own kind of strategy. It also means conduct matters: a short set of Fair Play Rules keeps every world fair, and a Report button handles anyone who breaks them.

Common early mistakes

  1. Picking a fight on day one. Opening against an established rival at a crowded hub splits demand you can't afford to split yet. Grow in open space first.
  2. Forgetting the clock. Over-expanding right before you log off can leave a fragile network running unattended for hours. Expand in steps you can check on.
  3. Single-threading into a contested market. Betting your whole airline on one route a rival also wants is fragile. Spread across a few markets where you're strong.
  4. Ignoring that rivals see you too. Your network, fares, and fleet are visible to other players. Assume they're scouting you the way you should be scouting them.

What comes next

Once the basics click — sign in, hub, fleet, routes, and the world loop — Headwinds opens up: connecting hubs, alliances and diplomacy, a loyalty program to lock in repeat flyers, debt to fund a well-timed expansion, and the long game of finishing a world on top. The Headwinds strategy guide goes deep on competing against real people, and the Fair Play Rules cover the ground everyone stands on.
