Devlog

What's changing in Headwinds, update by update. Headwinds shares its simulation engine with Tailwinds, so engine improvements — economy rebalances, the hub overhaul, loyalty rework, new aircraft — land here too; those are chronicled on the Tailwinds devlog. This page covers what's new in the multiplayer game itself.

July 17, 2026

One header, a searchable market, and sliders that slide

A round of interface upgrades driven by player feedback, focused on the Market, the cabin configurator, and the game's chrome.

  • One clean header. The separate bar above the game is gone. The tick countdown now lives under the date, and the lobby link, world feed, and messages sit in the game's own top bar — no more doubled-up Headwinds branding, and a full row of screen space back.
  • Search the aircraft market. A search box now sits above the filters — type a model or manufacturer and the list narrows instantly.
  • New table view for comparing aircraft. Switch the Market between photo cards and a compact table with sortable columns (seats, range, fuel per seat, efficiency, maintenance, lease and purchase price, delivery time). Click a column header to sort. Your choice is remembered.
  • Faster cabin configuration. Every class has plus/minus steppers and a Max button that fills the remaining floor space with that class in one click — an all-first-class cabin is one tap away. Each class also shows its revenue per unit of floor space, and new tooltips explain how seat quality (the hardware) differs from cabin classes (the layout).
  • Flight frequency shows its real limit. The route planner caps the flights-per-week slider at what one aircraft can physically fly on that distance (each airframe has 140 flying hours a week) and labels 7× as what it is: daily service. No more guessing why a long route won't take another rotation.
  • Sliders are actually draggable. The pay, maintenance, and frequency sliders had a tiny grab area that often started a text-selection drag instead. They now have a proper hit area and drag reliably.
  • Copy cleanup. Aircraft descriptions and interface text were rewritten to be shorter and plainer.

Spotted something clunky? Tell us on Discord — feedback like this is what shapes each update.

July 15, 2026

The world's routes, resized to reality

The demand model that decides how many passengers want to fly between any two cities has been recalibrated against real-world airline traffic data. The short version: the game's biggest markets are now the same ones real airlines fight over — and in a game where your rivals are other people, knowing which corridors are truly huge just became a real skill edge.

  • New giants on the map. Routes where flying is the only way — Seoul–Jeju, Tokyo–Sapporo, Sydney–Melbourne, Jeddah–Riyadh — now carry demand worthy of their real-world rank. If nobody in your world is flying them yet, that's an opening.
  • Borders, wealth and tourism now count. Domestic trunks outdraw same-sized international pairs, strong ties (UK–Ireland, Australia–New Zealand, Gulf corridors) keep cross-border demand high, tourist magnets like Cancún, Bali and Orlando draw on their visitors — and long thin routes between weakly connected cities are as marginal as they are in real life.
  • Bigger markets overall. Popular routes carry roughly 1.5–2x the passengers they used to, so expect stronger demand across your network and more room to add frequency — and expect your rivals to notice too.
  • Applies to everyone at once. Your world picks up the new demand at its next weekly tick, hitting every airline in the same moment — nobody gets a head start.

The full breakdown of what changed and why is on the Tailwinds devlog, where engine changes are chronicled.

July 14, 2026

Price every seat, right from the start

Fares used to be a two-step dance: set an economy price to open the route, then dig into the route list afterwards to price the premium cabins. No more — pricing is now one job, done up front.

  • Every cabin, priced before you fly. Opening a route now shows a fare for each cabin your aircraft actually carries — first, business, premium economy and economy — pre-filled at the market rate and ready to adjust before the first departure.
  • One fare editor everywhere. The same editor appears in the route planner, the add-flights form and the route list, with the market reference, your position against it, and a one-click reset. On the Routes tab, look for the Edit button in the Fares column.
  • Honest benchmarks. The reference fares shown for premium cabins were overstated relative to what passengers actually compare against — business and first looked further below market than they were. The benchmarks now match the demand model exactly, so a fare 10% over reference really is 10% over.
  • Forecasts you can trust. The planner's revenue and load projections now use the exact fares you've set, not assumed defaults — what you see before launch is what the route earns after it.

July 14, 2026

Scheduling without the guesswork

A round of quality-of-life work on putting aircraft where you want them.

  • Part-time aircraft can take new work. Route planners now offer any aircraft with spare weekly hours — not just idle ones — so a plane flying a short hop can pick up a second route without you juggling assignments.
  • Change frequency in place. Every route row has a −/+ stepper to add or drop weekly flights on the spot, and when an increase isn't possible it tells you exactly why — gate slots, block hours, or a regulatory cap.
  • Freight explains its ceiling. Long-haul freighters hit their weekly block-hour budget at roughly one rotation a day. Instead of a slider that silently stops, the cargo planner now says so — and points you at the real fix: add another freighter.

July 14, 2026

Seven new airports in Bangladesh

The map grows again: seven regional fields across Bangladesh — Ishwardi (IRD), Thakurgaon (TKR), Lalmonirhat (LLJ), Shamshernagar (ZHM), Comilla (CLA), Sirajganj (SAJ) and Khulna (KHL). Khulna anchors the set with a city of a million and a half; the rest are proper thin-route territory for turboprops feeding Dhaka. Domestic networks in the subcontinent just got a lot more interesting.

July 12, 2026

A dashboard that tells you why

The home dashboard has been rebuilt from a wall of numbers into a read on how your week actually went — and where the money went with it.

  • A weekly profit bridge. Last week's result sits next to what's projected for the week ahead, with the big movers — revenue, fuel, overhead — broken out so you can see exactly what changed.
  • Know which routes carry you. New True Profit and Last Week views sort your network by what each route really contributes, so the flights holding up the airline — and the ones dragging it down — are obvious at a glance.
  • The numbers that matter, with the story behind them. Load factor, yield, passenger satisfaction and fleet age each show a trend against last week, and every one opens a drill-down so you can chase a figure to its source.
  • World events you can inspect. Event chips open a detail panel, and the profit chart marks the weeks an event landed — so a surprising week is never a mystery.
  • Made to fit how you read it. Hover any chart for exact figures, switch the time range, and collapse the cards you don't need.

July 12, 2026

Cabins, re-priced

Fitting out your aircraft is clearer now, and the trade-offs are easier to reason about.

  • One less dial. The separate service-quality setting has been retired from aircraft and route setup — it wasn't a lever worth micromanaging plane by plane.
  • Seat quality, honestly priced. Standard economy seats are free, and new aircraft ship that way. Go above it and you'll pay a one-time cost to fit the cabin plus an ongoing cost to keep that standard — so a nicer seat is a real commitment, not a free upgrade.
  • Premium cabins cost to install. Adding premium economy, business, or first now carries a per-seat fitting fee — from $200 a seat up to $1,000 in first — reflecting the real cost of putting those seats in the aircraft.

July 12, 2026

Smoother worlds, truer numbers

A round of smaller additions and fixes to keep worlds fair and the figures trustworthy.

  • Worlds that start on the clock. A public world can now be set to open at a scheduled time, so a group can agree when a fresh world goes live and all join at the gun.
  • Connecting passengers, counted both ways. Routes are round trips, and traffic connecting over your hubs is now counted in both directions — so your connecting numbers reflect everyone you actually carry.
  • Polish throughout. The on-order delivery bar no longer sticks at zero, the maintenance-budget figure tracks its slider, cash runway matches the Finance page, route-map tooltips hold steady, and a handful of money and timing exploits are closed.

July 11, 2026

Your airline, charted over time

The Finance tab has a new Statistics page that turns your airline's history into charts — and lets you zoom from the last few weeks all the way out to decades.

  • Where your passengers come from. A stacked view splits every week's traffic three ways: travelers flying you point-to-point, passengers connecting over your own hubs, and the feed your alliance and codeshare partners hand onto your flights.
  • The numbers that matter, tracked. Revenue, costs and net profit; active routes, weekly departures, destinations served and fleet size; load factor, yield and capacity — each on its own chart, with details on hover.
  • Any window you like. Filter from the last 4 weeks up through 6 months and a year, and on out to 3, 5, 10, 20 or 30 years. Longer views smooth the line so a decade of flying still reads at a glance.
  • Builds as you fly. Your airline starts keeping these records the moment the update lands, so the picture grows richer with every week you play.

July 11, 2026

Keeping worlds fair: reports and bans

A world full of real people only stays fun if everyone plays straight. Headwinds now has real moderation built in.

  • Report a player. A report action is built into the Rivals list, your direct messages, and the Rules page — flag harassment, spam, or cheating and it goes straight to the moderators.
  • Account-wide bans. Moderators can remove a bad actor from every world at once, not just kick them out of one and watch them reappear in another.
  • The rules, one click away. A fair-play Rules page is now linked from the site nav and footer and from the in-game top bar, so what's allowed is never a mystery.

July 11, 2026

Scout your rivals

Competing against real people is far easier when you can read them. Three new views turn the other airlines in your world into usable intelligence.

  • Rival dossiers. Click any airline in the Rivals tab to open a full profile — its fleet, route network, performance trends, and a head-to-head comparison with you.
  • A real competitive map. The positioning chart now plots the actual human airlines in your world by price and reputation, instead of the made-up AI brands it used to show.
  • Market share, market by market. Airport and route views now show who holds what share of each city pair, so you can see exactly where you lead and where a rival has you beat.

July 11, 2026

Fly a route as often as you like

You've chosen the aircraft — now decide how hard to work it. Route detail gains a frequency control that puts weekly flights in your hands.

  • Set flights per week. Pick an aircraft on a route and dial its weekly frequency up or down, right from the route screen.
  • Watch utilization as you go. The card shows block-hour utilization live, so you can push an aircraft toward full use without overreaching.
  • The sim keeps you honest. The stepper only lets you go as far as your peak monthly block-hours, gate slots, and regulatory limits allow — and tells you which one you've hit.

July 11, 2026

Your first two aircraft, on day one

New airlines used to sit on the ramp waiting for deliveries. In Headwinds, your Starter Fleet is different.

  • Instant first deliveries. The first two aircraft you order arrive immediately — no watching a delivery queue before you can fly your first route.
  • Get in the game sooner. Stand up a network and start earning while the world is still young, instead of losing your opening weeks to the clock.

July 11, 2026

Sign in with Discord

Google was the quick way in; now there's a second one. You can sign in to Headwinds with Discord as well as Google — whichever account you already live in. It's still one account for all your worlds, on any device.

July 11, 2026

A new look

Headwinds has a mark of its own now — a plane climbing left into the head-on gusts, in Headwinds teal.

  • A logo of its own. The new mark shows up across the site and in the lobby header, with the favicon, social preview, and app icons all refreshed to match.
  • Teal, everywhere. Info pages and in-app footer links now carry the Headwinds wordmark and palette, so the whole thing reads as one product.

July 11, 2026

Talk to your rivals: in-game messaging

Competition is more fun when you can gloat, negotiate, or plead. Every world now has a messaging system built in — look for the ✉ Messages button in the game bar.

  • Direct messages. Write privately to any airline in your world. Propose a truce over a contested route, warn someone off your hub, or just talk shop.
  • Alliance chat. Every alliance gets a shared board only its members can read — coordinate hubs, split markets, plan the next move together.
  • Guardrails. Message limits keep spam down, and you can block any airline that won't take a hint — blocked senders can't reach you at all.

July 11, 2026

Player-founded alliances

Alliances in Headwinds aren't the scripted blocs from the solo game — there are no scripted anything in Headwinds. They're founded, grown, and run by players.

  • Found one from the world lobby. Name it and you're the founder. Other airlines request to join; you accept or reject them. Up to 8 active members.
  • Real benefits. Members feed each other connecting traffic, get a demand boost on routes where a partner also flies, and pay a weekly fee that keeps membership a real decision rather than a checkbox.
  • Real governance. If the founder walks away, leadership passes to the longest-serving member — or the alliance disbands. Choose your allies like it matters.

July 10, 2026

Humans are the competition

The biggest design decision in Headwinds, made permanent: there are no AI airlines. Every competitor on the map is a real person running a real network in your world.

  • Contested routes are player-vs-player. When two airlines fly the same city pair, the demand model splits passengers between them based on fares, frequency, quality, and loyalty — the same economics as always, but the other schedule belongs to a human who will react.
  • The Rivals tab. In multiplayer, the Competition tab becomes Rivals and shows the actual players in your world. The world standings do too — click any airline to open its profile: route network, fleet, alliance, and its recent moves, week by week.
  • No scripted drama. AI fare wars, scripted bankruptcies and startup carriers are all gone. If a fare war breaks out, it's because someone chose to start one.

July 10, 2026

Headwinds takes off

Headwinds is live at headwindsairlinegame.com — the multiplayer companion to Tailwinds, running the full airline simulation in persistent shared worlds.

  • Persistent worlds on a server clock. Game weeks advance automatically around the clock, whether you're online or not. Set up your network to survive without you, then come back to the results.
  • The whole game, multiplayer. This isn't a cut-down lobby experience — it's the complete Tailwinds interface (fleet, routes, hubs, finance, loyalty, cargo) running on server-authoritative state, so nobody can edit their save into a 747 fleet.
  • Fresh worlds, always. Public worlds spawn on a schedule and wait in the lobby at Year 1 until their first player joins — the clock starts when you do. Pick your pace, from a leisurely week per hour to faster worlds for the impatient.
  • One-click sign-in. Google or an email link. One account, all your worlds, from any device.
