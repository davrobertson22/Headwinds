Territory: Hub Strategy on a Shared Map

In solo airline games, a hub is an engineering project. In Headwinds it's a land claim. Every airport on the map is available to every player in the world, your rivals can see where you've planted your flag, and the best hub you'll ever have is one another player wanted too — but you got there first, dug in, and made attacking it pointless. This is a guide to thinking about hubs as territory.

The opening: claim before you optimize

A fresh world is a brief window where the map is empty and every player is making the same decision at once. Your starter fleet arrives instantly — Headwinds hands you your first two aircraft with no delivery wait, precisely so these opening moves happen at speed. Use that speed. The perfect spreadsheet-optimal hub chosen in week three is worth less than a very good hub claimed in week one, because by week three the very good hubs are taken.

What makes a claim defensible isn't raw airport size — it's the mismatch between how much the location is worth to you and how much it would cost a rival to contest. A dense catchment of nearby cities you can ring with short spokes, solid local demand to earn from before connections spin up, and no other player already committed there: that combination lets a modest airline become locked-in faster than a giant can be dislodged from anywhere.

Check the world feed and standings before you commit. If two players have already opened routes from the airport you're eyeing, you're not choosing a hub — you're choosing a battlefield.

Why fortresses hold

A developed hub defends itself through the connecting bank. Once a dozen spokes exchange passengers through your schedule, each spoke earns from dozens of onward markets that a point-to-point invader can't touch. A rival entering one of your spokes competes for that pair's local travellers only — while your aircraft on the same pair also carries everyone connecting to the rest of your network. You can profitably match a fare that loses them money, indefinitely. Most players learn to read this. The ones who don't, fund your flight academy.

This is also why the scariest attack on a hub isn't a route entry at all — it's a rival building their own hub one catchment over, then bleeding your outer spokes at their edges. Watch the map for construction, not just for fare cuts. The route economics guide covers the pricing mechanics of contested pairs; the short version is that geography wins arguments that fares can't.

Mega-hubs: the crowded crown

Every world has a handful of marquee airports that attract multiple players the way a gold rush attracts prospectors, and for the same reason: the prize is real, and so is the crowd. A shared mega-hub can work — the demand is enormous — but expect permanently contested spokes, thinner margins than the standings suggest, and rivals with an incentive to make your life expensive. Entering one late, against an established player whose bank structure is already humming, is the single most reliable way to torch a treasury in Headwinds. If you want a mega-hub, want it in week one with company, or take it in the endgame from a collapsing rival — not in the middle, alone, uphill.

The second hub is a race decision

In solo play, you open a second hub when the first one's finances say you're ready. On a shared map there's a second clock: the locations worth having are disappearing. That tension — expand before you're ready and risk the death spiral, or wait and watch the last good territory get claimed — is one of the sharpest decisions in the game, and there's no formula for it. What tilts it: how contested your region already is (crowded worlds reward earlier claims), how defensible your first hub is without your full attention, and whether the candidate site complements your network (a second hub whose catchment overlaps your first cannibalizes your own spokes — you want new geography, connected to the old by a fat trunk route).

However you time it, fund it from proven cash flow and connect it generously to hub one. Two well-linked hubs behave like one large network; two isolated ones behave like two vulnerable small airlines wearing the same livery.

Reading rivals' networks

Your competitors' hub choices are open information — the Rivals tab shows every airline's fleet and route map, and ten minutes studying them beats any general principle. Things worth knowing about each neighbour: where their bank structure actually is (that's the territory they'll defend to the death), which of their routes are stray singles (those they'll abandon under pressure), and whether their expansion is drifting toward your catchment. Alliances add a diplomatic layer on top — a neighbour you can't profitably fight is often a neighbour worth interlining with instead, turning an overlapping border into shared feed. See rivals & alliances for when to make that trade.

Endgame: territory becomes scoreboard

When the world's final weeks arrive, hub strategy inverts. Stop planting: a new hub that matures after the world ends is charity to the history books. Start harvesting: push fares on the spokes your fortress makes uncontestable, let marginal territory go rather than defending it at a loss, and watch for rivals who overextended — a player abandoning a hub dumps aircraft and vacates markets, and the standings reward whoever absorbs that traffic in the closing stretch. Your hub was never the goal. It was the machine that decides where you finish.
